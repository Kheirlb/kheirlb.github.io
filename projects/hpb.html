Helium Pressurization Bay

The Helium Pressurization Bay (HPB) was one of my first major projects as a member of the SDSU Rocket Project. I lead the design, assembly, and operation of a new multi-use remote helium pressurization system to safely pressurize the Galactic Aztec Heavy rocket safely from 200’ away.

I learned various aspects of fittings, valves, and pressure sensors and the associated systems engineering to put every component together. I learned the differences between and uses of globe valves, ball valves, needle valves, poppet valves, relief valves, vent valves, and more. I learned about hand loaded regulators and the benefit of liquid filled pressure gauges.

Several little rules of thumb I gained have become very handy in my work such as never add to many short tubes in a straight line and always do your best to have two bends in every length of tubing. The first rule of thumb allows for flexibility in assembly and more importantly, disassembly. The second allows some “give” during assembly or “play” for vibration and temperature affects.

System Operation General Description:

  1. A Helium K-Bottle is connected via a 10' 4000 psi perforated hose to the HPB inlet.
  2. Before opening the k-bottle, all valves are ensured closed and all regulators unloaded.
  3. Opening the pressure source allows helium to flow into the system, through a 15 micron filter, and into the first regulator with an inlet pressure gauge to show the gauge pressure of the source.
  4. For Galactic Aztec Heavy, the high pressure (~3000 psi) helium is then regulated down to 2000 psi.
  5. This Helium flows to a tee. The main line leads to high flow ball valve and the secondary line leads to another low pressure regulator.
  6. A 200' high pressure line is then connected between the HPB and the rocket. Once the main valve is opened, Helium moves and pressurizes the rocket.
  7. Once the rocket is pressurized, the main valve is closed and a vent valve is opened to vent the main line. A check valve on the rocket would prevent the rocket from depressurizing.
  8. At this point in time, the rocket is fueled and pressurized making it unsafe to approach.
  9. The secondary line and low pressure regulator allows the user to reugulate down further to 150 psi to flow through another 200' line.
  10. This second line is connected to the Helium Release Mechanism (HRM) which uses two actuators to pull down on a quick disconnect.
  11. The HRM disconnects the main line from the rocket.
  12. The rocket is ready to launch.
